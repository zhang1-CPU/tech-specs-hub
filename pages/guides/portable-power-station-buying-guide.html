Watt-hours, inverter ratings, MPPT solar charging, LiFePO4 vs lithium-ion — the jargon is everywhere. This guide breaks down every spec that actually matters, with real numbers from verified manufacturer data.

Step 1: Calculate Your Watt-Hour Needs

Capacity is the single most important number. It tells you how much total energy the battery holds. Think of it like a gas tank — a 2,000 Wh power station can theoretically run a 2,000 W device for 1 hour, or a 200 W device for 10 hours. In practice, inverter efficiency drops this to about 85-90%.

Laptop (charging) 45-85 W N/A ~80 Wh/h

The 30% Safety Margin Rule

Never size a power station to exactly match your calculated need. Always add 30% headroom. Why? Because: (1) inverter efficiency is 85-90%, not 100%; (2) battery capacity degrades over cycles; (3) real-world conditions are colder, which reduces lithium output; (4) startup surges briefly double the draw.

Needed: 1,500 Wh → Buy: 1,500 × 1.3 = 1,950 Wh (round to 2,000+ Wh unit)

Step 2: Make Sure AC Output Is Enough

Two numbers matter here: continuous output (what the inverter can sustain) and peak/surge output (what it can handle for 2-5 seconds during motor startup). Surge is critical for refrigerators, well pumps, and power tools.

Small: 500-1,000 W

Phones, laptops, LED lights, CPAP, mini fridge

Example: EcoFlow River 2 (600 W), Jackery 500 (500 W)

Medium: 1,500-2,500 W

Full-size fridge, microwave, TV, coffee maker

Example: Jackery 1500, EcoFlow Delta 2, Bluetti EB240

Large: 3,000-5,000 W

Well pumps, window AC, space heaters, multiple devices

Example: EcoFlow Delta Pro 3, Bluetti AC500

Beware the "Boost Mode" Fine Print

Some manufacturers (notably EcoFlow with "X-Boost") advertise that their units can "run" devices above the rated continuous output. This works by dropping voltage, which can damage or cause overheating in sensitive electronics like medical devices, power tool chargers, and modern refrigerators with variable-speed compressors. Always match the rated continuous output to your load, not the marketing "boost" number.

Step 3: LFP vs NMC — Battery Chemistry Deep Dive

This is the most important technical choice for long-term value. Both are lithium-based, but they behave very differently over time.

Property LiFePO4 (LFP) Li-ion (NMC/NCA)
Cycles to 80% capacity 3,000-6,000 cycles 500-1,000 cycles
Expected lifespan 8-12 years 3-5 years
Thermal runaway risk Very low Higher
Energy density (Wh/kg) 90-110 150-200
Weight for 2 kWh ~22 kg (48 lbs) ~15 kg (33 lbs)
Charge acceptance Excellent Good
Cold weather (-10°C) ~60% capacity ~70% capacity
Contains cobalt/nickel No Yes
Best for Daily cycling, off-grid, long-term investment Occasional use, portability, weight-sensitive

Recommendation

Unless portability/weight is your absolute #1 priority, choose LFP. The 6× longer cycle life means that over a 10-year ownership, an LFP unit actually costs less per usable Wh than an NMC unit, even if the sticker price is 20-30% higher today.

Step 4: Solar Charging — What MPPT, Voltage, and Current Actually Mean

Solar is what makes a "portable power station" into an energy system. Here's what to look for:

MPPT vs PWM — Always Choose MPPT

MPPT (Maximum Power Point Tracking) is a smart charge controller that squeezes 30-40% more charging power out of solar panels compared to the older PWM (Pulse Width Modulation) method. Every modern power station worth considering includes built-in MPPT — if it doesn't, skip it.

Solar Input Voltage Window

Check the MPPT voltage range. A unit accepting 12-150V gives you flexibility to wire panels in series for higher voltage and lower current (thinner cables, less loss). Narrow ranges like 12-30V force parallel wiring which creates cable-thickness problems at high wattages.

Combined AC + Solar Charge Speed

The best units can charge from AC wall outlet and solar panels simultaneously. EcoFlow advertises a "dual input" — wall AC + solar at the same time — which dramatically cuts charge time in mixed-source scenarios. This is a game-changer for grid-tied backup situations.

Real-World Solar Charge Times (1 kWh unit)

Step 5: Ports, Weight, and Practical Features

Must-Have Ports

  • AC outlets: At least 4 for versatility
  • USB-C PD (100W): Fast laptop/tablet charging without adapters
  • USB-A QC 3.0: Standard phone charging
  • 12V DC/Car port: Fridges, car electronics
  • Anderson/Solar input: For solar panel connection

Nice-to-Have Features

  • WiFi/Bluetooth app: Remote monitoring, firmware updates
  • Expandable batteries: Add capacity later without buying new inverter
  • UPS/EPS mode: Switch-over within 20ms for desktop computers
  • Display with SOC%: Battery State of Charge percentage (not just bars)
  • Low-temp charging cutoff: Protects LFP cells below 0°C/32°F

The Weight Tradeoff

Larger LFP units weigh 50+ lbs. For camping where you'll transport the unit weekly, cap at 500-1,500 Wh. For stationary home backup that moves once per season, 2,000+ Wh is fine. Consider wheeled cases for anything above 30 lbs that might need occasional transport.

Recommendations by Use Case

Home Backup for Power Outages

Minimum: 2,000 Wh capacity, 2,000 W continuous AC output, LFP battery.
Recommended: 4,000+ Wh with 3,000+ W output. Aim for a unit with UPS/EPS mode.

Top picks: EcoFlow Delta Pro 3, Bluetti AC500 + B300S

Weekend Camping / RV

Minimum: 500 Wh, 500 W output. 12V DC output essential for portable fridge.
Recommended: 500-1,500 Wh with 500-1,500 W output. Prioritize weight.

Top picks: Jackery Explorer 1000, EcoFlow River 2 Max

Off-Grid Cabin / Daily Use

Minimum: 3,000 Wh LFP, 1,200 W solar input minimum. Expandable architecture required.
Recommended: 6,000-12,000 Wh with 3,000+ W output. Multiple MPPT inputs.

Top picks: Bluetti AC200Max + expansion packs, EcoFlow Delta Pro Ultra

Common Spec Pitfalls to Avoid

  1. Peak wattage ≠ continuous wattage. A "3,000W peak" unit might only sustain 1,500W continuously. Always use the continuous rating.
  2. "110V outlet" doesn't mean the unit produces 110V. Some cheap inverters output modified sine wave, which damages power tool chargers, medical devices, and audio equipment. Look for "pure sine wave" — it's non-negotiable for anything above 300W.
  3. No-name brands on Amazon. Many list inflated wattage ratings and don't disclose cycle life. A "5,000W" $400 unit is using recycled laptop cells with 200-300 real cycles and poor thermal management.
  4. Ignoring the warranty. LFP cells have a 10-year lifespan, so warranty should reflect that. 2-year warranty on a 10-year-life product is a red flag.
  5. Buying the solar panel bundle at face value. Manufacturer "solar generator" kits bundle panels that are often undersized relative to the battery. Check the actual MPPT input capacity — you can usually add more panels later.
