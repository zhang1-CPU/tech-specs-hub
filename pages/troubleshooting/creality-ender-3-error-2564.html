ERROR 2564
Creality Ender 3 Heating Fault

Creality Ender 3 Error 2564 — Nozzle Not Heating Fix Guide

Error 2564 on the Creality Ender 3 indicates the firmware has detected a thermosensitive element failure, commonly referred to as a heating failure. This guide covers systematic diagnosis of the heating system including thermosensitive detection malfunction, heater cartridge resistance testing with reference values, thermistor resistance reference table, connector and wiring inspection, hotend assembly troubleshooting, and guidance on when to replace the heater cartridge versus the thermistor. Compatible with Ender 3, Ender 3 Pro, Ender 3 V2, Ender 3 S1, and Ender 3 S1 Plus.

What Usually Goes Wrong?

Error 2564 triggers when the firmware commands the hotend heater to turn on but does not detect the expected temperature rise within a set time window. The Creality firmware monitors both the heater cartridge (heating element) and the thermistor (temperature sensor). If the thermistor reports no temperature change after the heater is energized, or if the temperature reading is abnormal, the firmware assumes a fault and cuts power to prevent thermal runaway. The fault can originate from either the heating element itself or the temperature feedback loop.

  • • Heater cartridge failure: the heating coil inside the cartridge has burned out, creating an open circuit — the most common cause of no heat.
  • • Thermistor malfunction: the temperature sensor has failed, reading 0 ohms (shorted) or infinite ohms (open), causing the firmware to detect a fault even if heating is occurring.
  • • Loose or corroded connectors: the 2-pin connector at the hotend or the mainboard header has a poor connection, preventing current from reaching the cartridge.
  • • MOSFET failure: the switching transistor that controls the heater on the mainboard has failed, cutting power to the heater circuit entirely.

Work through these steps in order to systematically isolate the faulty component. Always power off the printer before accessing electrical connections.

Quick Symptom Check

Answer these before proceeding:

  • Does the hotend temperature display show 0°C or room temperature?
  • Does the temperature jump erratically or show 1000°C?
  • Did you recently change the nozzle, heat break, or hotend?
  • Did the error occur suddenly or gradually over many prints?
  • Is the printer powered by 12V or 24V supply?

Tools & Materials You'll Need

Electrical

  • Digital multimeter (resistance and continuity mode)
  • Replacement heater cartridge (24V 40W or 12V 40W depending on model)
  • Replacement NTC 100K thermistor (B3950 or B57560G)
  • Replacement 2-pin connector terminals (optional for repair)

Mechanical

  • Allen key set (1.5mm, 2mm for heat block set screws)
  • Heat-resistant gloves (for hot component handling)
  • Thermal paste or thermal compound (for thermistor seating)
  • Small brush (for cleaning heat block threads)

Safety

  • Power off and unplug printer before electrical diagnosis
  • Allow hotend to cool completely before disassembly
  • Heat-resistant gloves when working near heat block
  • Never operate printer with damaged wiring exposed

Diagnostic Tree — Work These In Order

01

Test heater cartridge resistance with a multimeter

The heater cartridge is the most likely culprit when the nozzle fails to heat. Power off the printer completely, then disconnect the heater cartridge wires from the mainboard. Set your multimeter to resistance mode (200 ohm range) and measure across the two heater cartridge terminals. A functioning 24V 40W heater cartridge should read approximately 14.4 ohms (±10%). A 12V 40W cartridge reads approximately 3.6 ohms. If you read infinite ohms (open circuit), the cartridge is burned out and must be replaced.

Expected Heater Cartridge Resistance
24V 40W cartridge: ~14.4 ohms (±10%)
12V 40W cartridge: ~3.6 ohms (±10%)
24V 30W cartridge: ~19.2 ohms (if equipped)
Open circuit: Infinite ohms = replace cartridge
02

Test thermistor resistance at room temperature

If the heater cartridge tests good, the thermistor is the next most likely failure. The Ender 3 uses an NTC (Negative Temperature Coefficient) 100K ohm thermistor with a B-value of 3950. At 25°C room temperature, it should read approximately 100,000 ohms (100K). If you read 0 ohms (shorted), infinite ohms (open circuit), or a value far outside the 100K range, the thermistor must be replaced. Use the reference table below to verify your reading matches the expected resistance at the measured ambient temperature.

03

Inspect all connectors and wiring end-to-end

Wiring and connector failures are common on the Ender 3 due to repeated X-axis flexing that stresses the wires near the hotend. Inspect the full circuit path: from the mainboard heater output connector, through the cable chain, to the 2-pin connector at the hotend. Check that the Dupont-style pins are not backed out of their housings, that there is no corrosion or oxidation on the metal terminals, and that the wires are not broken inside the insulation. A continuity test with a multimeter along each wire can identify intermittent breaks that are not visible.

Common failure points: the 2-pin JST connector at the hotend (wires can pull out), the mainboard header pins (loose crimps), and the point where wires enter the heat block (repeated bending causes conductor breakage inside insulation). Gently tug each wire at both ends — a loose connection will reveal itself.

04

Verify heater cartridge physical installation in heat block

A heater cartridge that is not fully seated in the heat block will have poor thermal contact, causing slow or inadequate heating. The cartridge should slide completely into the heat block hole and be retained by a small set screw. If the cartridge protrudes or rattles, remove it, clean any debris from the hole, and reinsert it fully before tightening the set screw. Apply a small amount of thermal paste to the cartridge tip before insertion to improve thermal conductivity to the block.

Heater Cartridge Installation Checklist
  • Cartridge fully inserted into heat block bore
  • Set screw tightened against cartridge body (not wires)
  • No gaps between cartridge and heat block
  • Apply thermal paste to cartridge tip before insertion
  • Wires secured and not touching heat block directly
05

Test MOSFET switching on the mainboard

If the heater cartridge and thermistor both test good and the wiring is intact, the MOSFET (Metal-Oxide Semiconductor Field-Effect Transistor) that switches the heater circuit may have failed. The MOSFET is the component on the mainboard that responds to the firmware command to energize the heater. A failed MOSFET will either not switch at all (open) or fail shorted (which is dangerous — heater stays on constantly). To test this without specialized equipment, you can measure for voltage at the heater output connector when the printer commands heating. If no voltage is present but the cartridge and wiring check out, the MOSFET likely needs replacement.

MOSFET failure symptom: if the heater stays on continuously even after turning off the printer (with power cable still connected), the MOSFET has failed shorted. This is a serious fire hazard — unplug immediately and do not use the printer until the MOSFET or mainboard is replaced.

06

Re-seat thermistor and check thermal contact

If the thermistor reads correct resistance but the firmware still reports a fault, the thermistor may not be making proper thermal contact with the heat block. An improperly seated thermistor will read ambient air temperature rather than block temperature, causing the firmware to detect that heating is not occurring. Remove the thermistor from its bore in the heat block, clean the bore with a small brush or needle, and reinsert the thermistor firmly. Apply a tiny amount of thermal compound if available. For screw-in type thermistors, ensure PTFE tape is applied to the threads to prevent shorting against the metal block.

Thermistor vs Heater Cartridge Decision Guide
  • Replace HEATER CARTRIDGE if: resistance is infinite (open) or far below spec
  • Replace THERMISTOR if: resistance is 0 ohms, infinite, or far from 100K at room temp
  • Replace BOTH if: wiring has been exposed to high heat damage or melted
  • Replace MAINBOARD if: MOSFET has failed shorted or ADC input is damaged
07

Re-run PID tuning after any heater or thermistor replacement

After replacing the heater cartridge or thermistor, you must re-run PID (Proportional-Integral-Derivative) tuning to calibrate the firmware's heating algorithm to your specific hotend configuration. The new components will have slightly different thermal characteristics, and without retuning, you may experience temperature overshoot, oscillation, or longer heat-up times. Use the appropriate firmware command for your firmware type and save the results to EEPROM.

  • Marlin: M303 E0 S200 C8 then M500 to save
  • Klipper: PID_CALIBRATE HEATER=extruder TARGET=200 then SAVE_CONFIG
  • Verify: after PID tuning, monitor temperature for 5 minutes to confirm stable holding within ±2°C of target

Firmware-Specific Notes

Creality Stock Firmware

Creality's stock firmware uses simple bang-bang control for the hotend heater rather than PID on some models. The firmware monitors temperature at regular intervals and energizes the heater when temperature drops below the target minus a hysteresis value. Error 2564 triggers when the temperature does not rise within the expected window. On stock firmware, the heating algorithm is not configurable — replacing faulty components is the primary fix.

Marlin Firmware

Marlin uses PID control for more stable temperature management. If you have flashed Marlin and are getting Error 2564, first run PID autotune (M303) to calibrate the PID values to your specific hotend. After any heater cartridge or thermistor replacement, the thermal characteristics change and the PID values must be updated. Verify your thermistor type is correctly defined in Configuration.h — wrong thermistor table selection causes incorrect temperature readings.

Klipper Firmware

Klipper uses a model-based heating control that monitors the thermal mass and heating rate. If Klipper reports a heater fault, run PID_CALIBRATE to retrain the model. The control algorithm in Klipper is more sensitive to accurate sensor readings, so a faulty thermistor will trigger errors faster than on Marlin. Check your Klipper printer.cfg for the correct sensor_type and pullup_resistor values.

Voltage Warning

The Ender 3 family includes both 12V and 24V models. Using a 12V heater cartridge on a 24V printer will result in insufficient heating (cartridge will produce only 1/4 of rated power). Using a 24V cartridge on a 12V printer will overheat and burn out the cartridge almost immediately. Always verify your printer's voltage before ordering replacement parts.

When to Call a Professional or Replace Parts

Heater cartridge shows infinite resistance — replace with correct voltage and wattage cartridge.

Heater stays on constantly even when printer is supposedly off — MOSFET has failed shorted, unplug immediately.

Thermistor reads 0 ohms or fluctuates wildly — replace with NTC 100K B3950 thermistor.

Mainboard shows visible burn marks or emits smoke — do not operate, replace mainboard.

If printer is under warranty and heating system shows fault, contact Creality support before self-repair.
