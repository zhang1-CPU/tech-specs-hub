Robot Vacuum Error Code Reference

These fault codes apply generically to most robot-vacuum brands (Roomba/iRobot, Roborock, Ecovacs/Dreame, Xiaomi, Neato). Exact wording varies by firmware — we use the most common phrasing. Always check the brand's official manual for your specific model.

Error Code Description Recommended Action Severity
Error 1 / Side Brush Side brush motor is jammed or the brush is tangled with hair/debris. Power off. Remove the side brush (single screw on most models). Clear debris from the brush hub. Check that the brush motor spins freely when manually turned. Reinstall and restart. Warning
Error 2 / Main Brush Main roller brush motor is stalled. Usually hair or string wrapped around the brush end-caps. Remove the main brush housing (two tabs on most Roborock/Roomba models). Pull out the brush. Clear hair and string from the end bearings — a seam ripper or the blade in the included cleaning tool speeds this up. Clean the end-cap bearings and reassemble. Warning
Error 3 / Left Wheel Left drive wheel encoder reports no motion. Wheel is physically stuck or the encoder is dirty. Flip robot. Manually rotate the left wheel to confirm it spins. If stiff, remove debris from the wheel well — pet hair is the #1 cause. If wheel spins freely but error persists, clean the optical encoder window (a small circular opening on the wheel motor) with isopropyl alcohol. Warning
Error 4 / Right Wheel Same as Error 3, mirrored to the right side. Same troubleshooting as Error 3. Note that on Roomba models, errors 3 and 4 sometimes both trigger simultaneously if the robot has been trapped and both wheels have stalled. Warning
Error 5 / Charging Charging contacts not making a good connection. Battery is present but not receiving charging current. Clean both the robot's bottom charging contacts and the charging base contacts with 99% isopropyl alcohol and a cotton swab. Confirm the charging base is plugged into a working outlet. If the error continues, try manually placing the robot on the base — sometimes the auto-docking alignment is slightly off. Info
Error 6 / Cliff Sensor Optical cliff sensor (on the bottom) is dirty or blocked. The robot refuses to drive because it thinks it's about to fall down stairs. Wipe all four or six cliff sensors (the small round lenses on the bottom) with a dry or slightly-damp microfiber cloth. Do not use abrasive cleaners. Dry the lenses completely before re-testing. On dark flooring, some robots are overly conservative — a firmware update sometimes fixes this. Info
Error 7 / Bin Full Dustbin optical sensor reports the bin is full. Some robots use an actual optical break-beam sensor; others estimate fill-level based on runtime. Empty the dustbin. Wash the dustbin with water (most are dishwasher-safe on the top rack — confirm your model). Dry thoroughly before reinserting. If error persists with an empty bin, the break-beam sensor lens on either side of the bin bay is dirty — clean with isopropyl alcohol. Info
Error 8 / Vacuum Fan Main suction fan is stalled or drawing too much current. Usually a blockage in the airflow path rather than a failed motor. Power off and remove dustbin. Check the suction inlet on the robot body for clogs (often a sock, paper, or large clump of hair). Check the internal airflow path from main brush compartment up through to the bin. If clear, the fan motor may be failing — listen for bearing noise during startup. Critical
Error 9 / Battery Low Battery voltage below the minimum threshold to start a cleaning run. Expected if the battery is truly discharged; unexpected if the robot has been on charge for several hours. Confirm robot is properly docked and charging (check for a charging indicator light). If the battery reports low immediately after a full charge cycle, the lithium-ion pack has likely reached end-of-life and must be replaced. Typical life is 300-500 full charge cycles. Warning
Error 14 / Water Tank Mop water tank is empty, not detected, or the filter screen inside the tank is clogged. Applies only to mopping models. Remove the water tank. Fill with clean water (room temperature). Check that the small filter screen inside the tank outlet is free of debris. Reinsert tank until it clicks. Some models require the mop pad to also be attached for the tank to be recognized. Info

Smart Thermostat Error Code Reference

These are manufacturer-specific fault codes for Nest and Ecobee. Honeywell T-series models use simpler "Equipment Error" codes; consult Honeywell's support site for those.

Nest Thermostat Faults

Nest E74 — Rh wire disconnected

The thermostat can't detect 24V on the Rh (heating-relay) terminal. Most common cause: a loose wire at either the thermostat or the furnace/air-handler control board. Also triggered if a fuse on the furnace control board has blown (typically a 3A or 5A automotive-style fuse).

Fix: Turn off furnace power. Remove thermostat faceplate. Confirm Rh wire is securely seated. Check furnace control board fuse. Restore power and re-test.

Nest E73 — Rc wire disconnected

Same as E74 but for the Rc (cooling-relay) terminal. If your system uses a single R wire (not split Rh/Rc), Nest expects the jumper between Rc and Rh to be in place. Missing the internal jumper is the single most common DIY-install cause of E73.

Fix: Confirm the R wire is in the correct terminal. For a single-R system, confirm the Nest wiring base has its built-in Rh-Rc jumper connected (Nest Learning has this; Nest Thermostat 2020+ uses a separate plug-in jumper).

Nest E194 — Wi-Fi connection lost

Nest can't reach Google's servers. Almost always a local Wi-Fi issue, not a Nest hardware failure.

Fix: Restart your Wi-Fi router. Restart the thermostat (Settings > Restart). Confirm 2.4 GHz SSID is visible (Nest does not connect to 5 GHz-only networks). Confirm your router isn't blocking IoT ports or using AP isolation.

Nest H71 — Heating relay failure

Nest has detected that its internal heating relay is stuck in either the on or off position. This is a hardware fault.

Fix: Unit must be replaced. Nest warranties are 1-2 years depending on model. If out of warranty, the thermostat can sometimes still function in cooling-only mode temporarily, but the heating circuit is unreliable and should not be trusted.

Nest C155 — Compressor communication

On heat-pump systems, Nest detects an unexpected signal from the compressor or a mismatch between what Nest commanded and what the system reports back.

Fix: Check the outdoor unit. Confirm the reversing valve is switching correctly (often the clicking solenoid can be heard from outside). Check defrost cycle timing. If outdoor unit looks normal, re-confirm Nest's heat-pump configuration (O/B terminal polarity, stage count).

Ecobee Thermostat Faults

Equipment Stages Mismatch

Ecobee has detected that the number of heating or cooling stages it's configured for doesn't match what it's seeing on the wire terminals. Common after a firmware update that re-detects wiring.

Fix: Go to Main Menu > Settings > Installation Settings > Equipment > Wiring. Manually re-confirm each detected terminal. If the HVAC system is actually 2-stage but configured as 1-stage (or vice versa), the thermostat will misbehave silently — confirm against the furnace/heat-pump model.

Sensor Not Detected

An Ecobee remote room sensor (temperature + occupancy) isn't communicating. These communicate on a proprietary sub-1 GHz protocol, not Wi-Fi.

Fix: Replace the CR2032 coin-cell battery in the sensor (typical life ~12-18 months). If still not detected, the sensor may be out of range — Ecobee quotes 45 ft / 15 m through typical interior walls. Move the sensor closer to the thermostat, or add a second sensor to extend effective range (they mesh). If all sensors fail simultaneously, the radio in the thermostat itself may have failed.

High Humidity Alert

Indoor humidity reading is above your configured threshold (defaults vary by model, typically 60%). Usually not a fault, but an environmental alert.

Fix: Confirm the humidity reading with a secondary meter (cheap digital hygrometers work fine). If Ecobee reading is 10+ percentage points off, re-baseline the humidity sensor in Settings > Sensors. If reading matches, run a dehumidifier or confirm your AC is functioning (AC naturally dehumidifies).

Auxiliary Heat Running Too Long

Applies only to heat-pump systems with auxiliary (electric-resistive or gas) backup heat. Ecobee detects that the aux heat has been running for more than your configured threshold (default is 3 hours in a 24-hour period), indicating the heat pump is unable to keep up.

Fix: First confirm outdoor conditions — at temperatures below the heat-pump's balance point (often 30-35 °F / 0-2 °C), auxiliary heat is normal and expected. If the weather is mild, inspect the outdoor heat-pump unit for a stuck reversing valve, low refrigerant (requires a technician), or a failed defrost cycle.

Smart Lighting Protocol Comparison

Zigbee

Low-power mesh protocol operating in the 2.4 GHz ISM band. Used by Philips Hue, IKEA Tradfri, Osram/Sylvania, and most professional lighting systems.

  • Power: very low, ~0.5 W standby per bulb
  • Hub required: yes (Zigbee coordinator)
  • Local control: yes, within the mesh
  • Range per node: ~30 ft / 10 m interior

Wi-Fi

Direct Wi-Fi connection. Used by Lifx, Kasa, Meross, Wiz (some). No hub required, at the cost of higher per-device power consumption and heavier load on your Wi-Fi network.

  • Power: higher, ~2-3 W standby per bulb
  • Hub required: no
  • Local control: varies — some brands require cloud round-trip
  • Range per node: your Wi-Fi coverage

Thread / Matter

Thread is an IP-based mesh protocol (802.15.4 at the physical layer, same radio as Zigbee but different networking stack). Matter is the application-layer standard that can run over Thread, Wi-Fi, or Ethernet.

  • Power: very low, comparable to Zigbee
  • Hub required: a Thread Border Router is required (many newer Apple, Google, and Amazon devices serve this role automatically)
  • Local control: yes, by design — Matter is local-first
  • Range per node: ~30 ft / 10 m interior
