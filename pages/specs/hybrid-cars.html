Battery Chemistry Explained

Three chemistries dominate the hybrid & EV landscape — each with distinct trade-offs in temperature tolerance, cycle life, and thermal stability.

NiMH (Nickel-Metal Hydride)

The workhorse chemistry in Toyota and Toyota/Lexus hybrids. Proven reliability, proven in Toyota hybrids. The proven hybrid technology with lower energy density but excellent thermal runaway risk near zero.

Temperature range-30°C to +60°C
Cycle life1,500-3,000
Self-discharge~20%/month
Thermal runawayVery Low

Li-ion (NCA / NMC)

High energy density — Tesla's legacy chemistry. Higher voltage and lighter weight, but requires careful thermal management and BMS oversight.

Temperature range-20°C to +45°C
Cycle life1,500-2,500
Self-discharge~2-3%/month
Thermal runawayMedium-High

LFP (LiFePO4)

Tesla's standard-range chemistry (Gigafactory Shanghai). Exceptional cycle life — 3,000-4,000 full cycles — and inherent thermal stability, but lower energy density.

Temperature range-20°C to +60°C
Cycle life3,000-4,000
Self-discharge~2-3%/month
Thermal runawayLow

Nominal Voltage & Module Arrangement

How the Toyota hybrid battery pack is assembled — and why the module voltage numbers matter for diagnostics.

Every Toyota hybrid battery pack is built from identical 7.2V modules. Each module contains 6 individual NiMH cells wired in series, each producing approximately 1.2V nominal.

These modules are then wired in series to produce the pack's total nominal voltage. The higher the pack voltage, the more power the hybrid system can deliver — but the more modules you need to monitor.

Prius Gen 2 / 3 Pack Voltage
28 modules × 7.2V = 201.6V

The Camry and RAV4 Hybrid use a higher-voltage arrangement: 34 modules at 7.2V each, producing a nominal pack voltage of 244.8V. Prius Gen 4 steps up to 207.2V.

Why LFP?

Tesla's shift to LFP for standard-range vehicles trades some energy density for dramatically better cycle life — 3,000+ full cycles vs ~1,500 for NCA — and eliminates cobalt from the supply chain.

The pack uses a 4680 structural pack architecture with cells bonded directly into the pack structure, reducing mass and improving crash safety. The 1P96S configuration means 96 cells in series, producing the 380V nominal voltage.

Diagnostic note: Tesla's BMS reports individual cell voltages. Any cell showing >50mV deviation from the pack median indicates a faulty module requiring replacement.

OBD-II Hybrid Battery Fault Codes

The most common diagnostic trouble codes (DTCs) related to hybrid battery packs — each linked to our full troubleshooting guides.

P0A80 Critical

Replace Hybrid Battery Pack

Overall hybrid battery pack has reached end-of-life threshold. Internal resistance too high, capacity below 70%. Full pack replacement or module-by-module rebuild.

P0A7F Warning

HV Battery Module Balance Fault

Individual module voltage spread exceeds tolerance. One or more modules weakening. Identify via voltage scan, rebalance or replace affected modules.

View troubleshooting guide →
P0A7E Warning

Battery Current Sensor Circuit

Current sensor reading out of range — often a loose or corroded sensor connection at the battery ECU. Check harness and sensor calibration.

Technical reference
P0A94 Warning

Battery Temperature Sensor Circuit

Battery pack temperature sensor fault. The battery ECU cannot read one or more temperature sensors. Check harness continuity and sensor resistance.

Technical reference
P0A78 Critical

HV Battery System Malfunction

General battery system malfunction. Typically occurs with other codes — scan for additional DTCs to pinpoint root cause before proceeding with diagnostics.

Technical reference
P0A1F Warning

Drive Motor "A" Position Sensor Circuit

MG1 or MG2 position sensor signal fault. Often misaligned or damaged resolver. Replace sensor; check wiring to inverter — requires Techstream calibration.

Technical reference

Battery Control System Malfunction

Battery ECU internal malfunction. Often accompanies other codes — check for loose main relay, corrosion, or faulty ECU communication.

DIY Tools for Hybrid Battery Diagnosis & Repair

The right tools make the difference between a successful rebuild and a wasted weekend.

Digital Multimeter

CAT III rated, 1000V. Measure individual module voltages — your primary diagnostic tool for identifying weak modules.

Must be true-RMS

Infrared Thermometer

Spot-check module temperatures under load. Hot modules = high internal resistance = failing.

-50 to +500°C range

Hybrid Battery Load Tester

Applies controlled discharge load and measures voltage sag per module. Identifies weak modules under real load.

Aftermarket units available

Insulated Gloves & Eye Protection

Class 0 rated (1000V). Working on 200+V hybrid packs is not a hobby — insulate and isolate.

1000V rated minimum

OBD-II Scan Tool (Techstream or equivalent)

Reads hybrid-specific PIDs — module voltages, temperatures, current, and state of charge in real time.

Supports enhanced diagnostics

10mm & 12mm Socket Set

The most-used tools for disassembly. Toyota hybrid packs use these almost exclusively for module fasteners.

6-point preferred

Battery Module Charger

Individual 7.2V module charger for initial equalization after module replacement. Critical before reassembly.

Constant-current / constant-voltage
