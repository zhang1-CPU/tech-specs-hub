Off-Grid Solar System Sizing Guide

The actual math behind sizing a solar + battery system that works in your real-world location — not the marketing numbers.

Critical: Generic Sizing Advice Will Leave You Without Power

Solar system sizing depends on your specific location, weather patterns, worst-case winter sun hours, and actual daily consumption. Generic "5kW = 5 panels" advice is dangerous — it leaves you without power during extended cloudy weather. This guide walks you through the real formulas.

Step 1

Calculate Daily Energy Consumption

Everything starts with what you actually use. Be honest — lowballing here ruins every other number downstream.

Device Inventory Worksheet

List each device you plan to run, its wattage, and how many hours per day it operates. For devices that cycle (refrigerator, well pump), estimate average run time — not just on-time. Use a Kill-A-Watt meter or the nameplate wattage printed on the device.

Device Wattage (W) Hours / Day kWh / Day
Refrigerator (18 cu ft, cycle) 150 W 8 hrs 1.20 kWh
LED Lighting (living + bedroom) 60 W 5 hrs 0.30 kWh
Laptop computer 50 W 4 hrs 0.20 kWh
Well pump (½ HP, intermittent) 500 W 1 hr 0.50 kWh
Space heating (resistive) ← #1 load driver 1,500 W 3 hrs 4.50 kWh
Microwave 1,000 W 0.25 hrs 0.25 kWh
Modem + Wi-Fi router 25 W 24 hrs 0.60 kWh
Phone charging (2 devices) 20 W 2 hrs 0.04 kWh
TV + streaming box 120 W 3 hrs 0.36 kWh
Miscellaneous (small appliances) 200 W 2 hrs 0.40 kWh
SUBTOTAL — — 8.35 kWh / day
× 1.30 efficiency factor (inverter losses, wire drop, battery efficiency) — — × 1.30
PLANNED DAILY CONSUMPTION — — ~10.86 kWh / day
Why the 1.30 efficiency factor? Real-world losses come from: the inverter (~90-95% efficient), voltage drop in wiring between panels and charge controller, battery round-trip efficiency (~85-90% for LiFePO4), and dust/heat-related panel derating over time. 1.25–1.35 is a realistic safety margin.

Formula

Daily Consumption (kWh) = Σ (device watts × hours per day) ÷ 1,000
Planned Daily = Daily Consumption × 1.30 (inefficiency safety factor)
Step 2

Determine Autonomy / Backup Days

How many cloudy days in a row do you need to survive without sun?

Off-Grid Cabin

3-5 days

Permanent off-grid — grid is not an option. Size for the longest cloudy stretch you'd expect in your location's worst season.

Use 3–5 days minimum; 5–7 for northern latitudes.

Grid-Tied Backup

1-2 days

Whole-home battery backup where the grid is normally available. Outages are measured in hours to a few days. Solar is optional.

Use 1–2 days. Add more if frequent multi-day storms.

Cold Climate / Winter

5-7 days

Short sun hours, heavy heating load, occasional snow covering panels. Northern US, Canada, and similar climates.

Use 5–7 days — worst-case scenario sizing.
Rule of thumb: most installers use 3 days as a baseline for off-grid homes. This means the battery bank alone can power your full load for 3 full days without any solar input. Combined with the array, the system should handle 1–2 weeks of cloudy-but-not-totally-dark weather.
Step 3

Size the Battery Bank

Battery capacity depends on daily use, days of autonomy, and the safe depth-of-discharge of your chemistry.

Battery Sizing Formula

Battery Capacity (kWh) = Daily Consumption × Autonomy Days ÷ DoD
DoD = Depth of Discharge (LiFePO4: 0.80 — Lead-acid: 0.50 — Lithium-ion NMC: 0.70)

Worked Battery Bank Calculation

Planned daily consumption (from Step 1) 10.86 kWh
× Autonomy days (3) × 3
÷ LiFePO4 DoD (0.80) ÷ 0.80
Required battery bank 40.73 kWh
Rounded up to nearest standard pack size ~42 kWh usable (6 × 7 kWh packs)
Voltage Tier
48V

Standard for systems above 5 kWh. Lower current = thinner wire = cheaper. Avoid 12V for whole-home systems.

Chemistry
LiFePO4

Cycle life 3,000–6,000, 80% DoD. 2026 pricing makes this the default for all systems.

Note that lead-acid appears cheapest per-kWh of nameplate capacity, but because it requires oversizing by ~60% (to stay above 50% state-of-charge), the real cost per-usable-kWh is comparable to LiFePO4 — and the cycle life is 1/5 as long. On a levelized cost basis, LiFePO4 is the cheapest option.

Step 4

Size the Solar Array

Your array must recharge the full battery bank on the worst sun day of the year — in December, not July.

Solar Array Sizing Formula

Solar Array (kW) = Daily Consumption (kWh) × 1.15 ÷ Peak Sun Hours
1.15 = inefficiency factor (wiring losses, panel derating, MPPT efficiency, temperature derating)
Final Array Size = Calculated Array × 1.25 (cloudy / seasonal oversizing factor)

Peak Sun Hours by US Location (Worst Month)

Peak Sun Hours (PSH) = equivalent hours of 1,000 W/m² irradiance per day. This is the industry standard way to compare locations. Always use the December average (or your worst month) — not the yearly average.

Step 5

Size the Inverter / Charger

The inverter must handle simultaneous loads — and its surge rating must start the biggest motor-driven device.

Continuous Rating

Sum up all devices that could realistically run simultaneously. This is not every device in your inventory — it's the realistic worst-case combination (e.g., fridge + well pump + microwave + lights, but not all at once unless you genuinely do that). Then multiply by 1.25 as a safety factor.

Fridge + well pump + microwave + lights + modem ~2,400W
× 1.25 safety factor × 1.25
Minimum continuous inverter rating 3,000W

Surge Rating (Often the Limiting Factor)

Motor-driven devices draw 3–7× their rated wattage for 0.5–3 seconds at startup. Identify the single biggest motor load and ensure the inverter's surge rating exceeds that figure.

DeviceRunning (W)Startup Surge (W)Requires Inverter Surge
Well pump (½ HP) 500 2,500–3,500 6,000W+ surge
Refrigerator (18 cu ft) 150 1,200–1,800 3,000W+ surge
Window AC (8,000 BTU) 900 3,000–5,000 8,000W+ surge
Central AC (3 ton) 2,800 8,000–14,000 18,000W+ surge

Inverter recommendation for our worked example: a 6,000W continuous / 12,000W surge hybrid inverter (commonly available as a single 48V all-in-one unit). This handles the well pump's startup surge while comfortably running the full house load.

Charge Controller (MPPT) Sizing

Size the MPPT charge controller to handle the full short-circuit current from the array. Formula:

MPPT Current (A) = Array Wattage ÷ Battery Voltage × 1.25

For a 4,950W array on a 48V battery: 4,950 ÷ 48 × 1.25 = 129A. A 150A MPPT controller provides adequate margin. Many modern hybrid inverters integrate MPPT charging internally, so you may not need a separate controller.

Step 8

Common Sizing Mistakes to Avoid

These are the reasons off-grid and backup solar systems fail to deliver what the owner expected.

1

Using summer / annual average sun hours instead of December worst-case

The system needs to work in December, not July. In many northern US locations, December solar production is 40–50% of the annual average on a per-day basis — and days are shorter. If you size to the annual average, you'll run out of power in the darkest months.

2

Under-sizing battery autonomy (1 day vs. 3–5 days)

A 1-day backup battery means any multi-day storm leaves you without power. Multi-day cloud cover + snow-on-panels is the worst case that off-grid and home-backup systems must survive. Budget at minimum 3 days of autonomy for off-grid, and scale up from there based on how much you value reliability.

3

Forgetting inverter surge rating

A 3,000W continuous inverter may trip on a 500W well pump that draws 3,500W at startup. Surge rating is the single most important spec for motor-driven loads. Always size surge to 3–5× your biggest motor device rating.

4

Ignoring wire gauge / voltage drop

Thin wire between panels and charge controller / battery can waste 10%+ of production as heat. Between the battery bank and the inverter, wire too thin drops voltage, reduces inverter efficiency, and is a fire risk. Use an online wire-size calculator (based on distance and current) and then go one size larger than the calculator suggests.

5

Flat-mounted panels / poor tilt and azimuth

Mounting flat on a horizontal roof is convenient, but it can cost you 30%+ of production in winter compared to a proper tilt. Optimal tilt = your latitude ± 5°. Azimuth should be true south (180°) for fixed panels. East-west splits are acceptable for rooftop layouts where full south isn't possible, but expect ~10% loss.

6

Assuming 100% depth-of-discharge on batteries

Never plan to drain the battery to zero. LiFePO4: 80% DoD is safe and maximizes cycle life. Li-ion/NMC: 70% DoD. Lead-acid: 50% DoD. Pushing past these is the #1 reason battery packs fail early. Use these conservative numbers in your sizing math from the start.

7

Mixing incompatible components from different brands

Not all inverters can charge from all panels and all battery chemistries. Voltage ranges, communication protocols (CANbus, RS485, proprietary), charge profiles, and ground-fault protection must all match. Buy as much of the system from a single integrated ecosystem as possible — it saves weeks of debugging and is more reliable long-term.
